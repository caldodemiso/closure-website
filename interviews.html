Interview 1 – Known Contact
Name:
Daniel Buonavita
Title / Role:
Test Engineer at Revelyst
Date of Meeting:
Monday, December 1, 2025
Selection Rationale
I selected Daniel because he is one of my project stakeholders, he knows my technical background and growth firsthand, and he has real-world experience with engineering, networking, and project management. He was able to give meaningful feedback on both the technical direction of my homelab and on my approach to project planning and execution.
Conversation Summary

Daniel explained how project management has shaped both his professional and personal life. With a background in industrial engineering, he managed production lines where planning, alignment, and resource management were critical. He emphasized that project management is not just a work skill but a mindset that improves decision-making everywhere, including personal projects like home remodels. He described how breaking down large goals into small, manageable pieces creates clarity and prevents overwhelm.

He then shared how he personally uses project management to maintain alignment with his team, identify blockers early, and ensure transparency. He stressed that the value of a project plan increases when resources are limited because planning prevents waste. These points connected directly to my homelab project, where time, budget, and hardware limitations required intentional planning.

Next, Daniel offered advice for students learning project management. He warned against becoming overly focused on tools—Jira, Monday, ClickUp—and losing sight of the purpose of project management. Tools support the process but should not dominate it. He said flexibility is essential because projects always evolve.

He also shared hiring insights. The two qualities he values most in candidates are strong communication and a growth mindset. Strong communication ensures alignment and prevents issues from escalating, while a growth mindset makes someone adaptable, open to learning, and capable of long-term growth.

Daniel closed by reminding me not to overthink project planning. He explained that simple tools like whiteboards and sticky notes are often enough. What matters is clarity, simplicity, and momentum—not complexity.

Key Insights
  • Momentum matters more than perfection
  • Tools don't make a project manager—mindset does
  • Communication is foundational for effective teamwork
  • Growth mindset predicts long-term success
  • Breaking large goals into small steps prevents overwhelm
  • Plans should adapt as information changes
